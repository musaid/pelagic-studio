All values are direct microspectrophotometry (MSP) measurements on adult _Thunnus albacares_ retinal tissue.

## Evidence

This is the most robustly characterized species in our system. The data comes from direct MSP measurements on the species itself.

**Key findings from Loew et al. (2002):**

- Adult YFT are **photopic dichromats** with two cone pigment types
- **Twin cones** (λmax = 485 nm) are the dominant photoreceptor, responsible for most brightness and motion detection. These are blue-green sensitive.
- **Single cones** (λmax = 426 nm) provide secondary short-wavelength discrimination in the violet range.
- **No long-wavelength cones** — red, orange, and most yellow are functionally invisible.
- Peak sensitivity sits in the 420-500 nm range (violet through blue-green).
- Rod pigment (λmax = 483 nm) is nearly identical to the twin cone peak, consistent with a deep-ocean visual ecology where both scotopic and photopic vision are tuned to the same blue-green ambient light.

## Ecological Context

Yellowfin tuna are epipelagic predators inhabiting the upper 100m of the open ocean. The water column in their habitat is dominated by blue light (460-500 nm), and their visual system is precisely matched to this environment.

Their dichromatic vision is sufficient for detecting prey silhouettes against the blue background and for discriminating the contrast patterns of baitfish. The absence of long-wavelength sensitivity is not a disadvantage in their habitat — red light is almost entirely absorbed within the first 10-20m of open ocean water.

For lure design, this means:

- Blue, violet, and metallic finishes maintain visibility at all depths
- Red and orange are functionally invisible, especially below 10m
- Green is partially visible but dims rapidly with depth

## Gaps

None significant. This species has the strongest direct evidence of any in our system. Future work could characterize the full retinal mosaic topography and cone-to-ganglion-cell ratios for a more detailed understanding of spatial resolution across the visual field.
