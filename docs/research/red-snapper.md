## Evidence

### Primary source — Lythgoe et al. (1994)

This is the definitive MSP study on snapper visual pigments, examining 12 *Lutjanus* species from the Great Barrier Reef:

**Key findings:**
- All species have **double cones with spectrally distinct members** — each half of the double cone contains a different visual pigment (RH2 + LWS)
- **Single cones** contain shorter-wavelength SWS2 pigment
- Rods cluster around **492-502 nm**
- Double cone pigments: one member ~517-530 nm (RH2), the other ~540-565 nm (LWS)
- **Systematic spectral shifting** based on habitat water color:
  - Outer reef (clear blue water) species → shorter-wavelength double cone pigments
  - Inshore/mangrove (green-stained water) species → longer-wavelength shifted pigments
  - This is a textbook demonstration of Lythgoe's sensitivity hypothesis

### Supporting — *Lutjanus erythropterus* (crimson snapper) genomics

Opsin gene analysis (2021, *Gene*) identified 32 opsin genes:
- **SWS2:** 3 copies (sws2.1, sws2.2, sws2.3)
- **RH2:** 4 copies (rh2.1-rh2.4)
- **LWS:** 1 copy
- **RH1:** 1 copy
- **No SWS1 (UV)** — gene absent from genome entirely
- All visual opsins highly expressed in retina

This confirms the trichromatic cone complement at the genomic level and rules out UV sensitivity.

### Supporting — gray snapper (*Lutjanus griseus*) ERG

McComb et al. (2013) measured electroretinographic spectral response:
- Response range: 360-620 nm
- Multiple cone pigments confirmed by ERG waveform shape
- Highest critical flicker fusion frequency: 47 Hz (fast visual processing, adapted for diurnal bright environments)
- Shortest photoreceptor response latency: 26.7 ms

### Derivation of red snapper values

- **SWS2 (440 nm):** The SWS2 opsin class in percomorphs typically tunes to 397-482 nm. For reef Lutjanidae, ~440 nm is consistent with Losey et al. (2003) survey data for reef fish single cones.
- **RH2 (520 nm):** Lythgoe et al. (1994) measured double cone member 1 at ~517-530 nm across species. 520 nm represents the outer-reef / clear-water end of this range, appropriate for *L. campechanus* which inhabits Gulf of Mexico reef structures in relatively clear water.
- **LWS (555 nm):** Lythgoe et al. (1994) measured double cone member 2 at ~540-565 nm. 555 nm is a mid-range estimate.
- **Rods (497 nm):** Directly supported by the MSP consensus of 492-502 nm for Lutjanidae.

## Ecological Context

Red snapper are reef-associated predators that inhabit structured habitats (natural reefs, artificial reefs, oil platforms) at depths of 10-130m. Their trichromatic vision is a **major departure from the dichromatic pelagic predators** (tuna, GT):

- **LWS cone (555 nm)** provides sensitivity to green-yellow wavelengths that tuna cannot see at all. This means:
  - Green and yellow lure colors are **visible** to snapper
  - Orange is partially visible at shallow depths
  - Red is still attenuated rapidly with depth (water physics) but the fish can perceive it near the surface
- **Three-channel color discrimination** enables snapper to detect prey against varied reef backgrounds with more nuance than dichromats
- **Spectral tuning to water color** (Lythgoe et al. 1994) suggests snapper visual pigments are adapted to match their local water conditions for optimal contrast detection

For lure design:
- Green and chartreuse lures are effective — well within snapper visual sensitivity
- Red and pink can work at shallow depths but lose effectiveness rapidly below 10-20m
- Blue and metallic remain universally visible
- The broader color gamut means more lure colors "work" for snapper than for tuna

## Gaps

- **No direct MSP on *Lutjanus campechanus*.** The Lythgoe et al. (1994) study used Australian GBR *Lutjanus* species. The visual pigments of Western Atlantic red snapper may differ slightly due to different water conditions (Gulf of Mexico vs. GBR). The spectral shift with water color documented by Lythgoe et al. suggests this is plausible.
- **Full data tables from Lythgoe et al. (1994) are behind a paywall.** The specific per-species λmax values for all 12 Lutjanus species would allow more precise estimation. The ranges cited here are from the abstract and secondary literature.
- **No data on *L. campechanus* lens transmission.** The UV-blocking properties of the lens (which determines whether UV light reaches the retina) have been measured for some reef fish but not specifically for red snapper.
